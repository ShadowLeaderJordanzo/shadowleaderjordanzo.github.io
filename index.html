Stanley Milgram

Stanley Milgram was a Jewish born Psychologist from the Bronx, New York. He was noted as one of the most important social psychologists of the 20th century. He was known for his experiments on obedience and authority. He was born in 1933 and died in 1984.
Milgram's most famous experiment was the Obedience Experiment, an experiment designed to study the obedience of people to authority figures. Milgram had devised his study to try and explain the psychology of genocide, and how people could be so easily persuaded to commit atrocities.
In his own words: "Could it be that Eichmann and his million accomplices in the Holocaust were just following orders? Could we call them all accomplices?"
The results of the experiment were shocking, and showed that people were willing to obey authority figures even when they were told to commit acts that they knew were wrong. The original researchers estimated a fraction of a tenth of a percent of participants would disobey the experimenter, but in reality, 65% of participants obeyed the experimenter and continued to administer shocks to the learner.

Miligram faced harsh critizism about how ethical his experiment was after it was published. These ethical concerns were because of the deception used, the lack of protection of the participants and the fact that the right to withdraw was seemingly ignored.
Due to the controversy surrounding him his contract at Yale was only extended for a year, but he was also made a lecturer when before he was an assistant professor. Despite the bad publicity, Milgram's study is still considered one of the most important experiments in psychology. He also went on to publish many other studies, including the famous "Small World Experiment" which showed that the average number of connections between two people is 6.

Miligram 18 has been reproduced and referenced multiple times in media.

The Milgram Experiment

Similar to the fake experiment made up in an earlier slide, this involves a teacher and a student. The Milgram Experiment involved a teacher and a learner who were both assumed to be coming to the lab for a psychology experiment. One of these people was an actor, and the other was a real participant, the Experiment was also rigged so that the actor would always be the learner. There was also an Experimenter who was also an actor, and was in charge of the experiment. The Experimenter would tell the teacher that the learner was in the next room, and that they were going to be administering shocks to the learner and that the research was about the effects of punishment on learning. If the learner was wrong, the teacher would have to admister increasing levels of shock to the learner; the learner who was an actor would always be wrong and would always cry out in pain. As the shocks got increasingly more painful, the learner would beg the teacher to stop, but the teacher would be told by the experimenter that it must continue until the learner got the answer right. When asked if the shocks would leave any damage, the Experimenter would assure them that the shocks were completely safe, just temporary discomfort. Once the voltage got to around the 300 volt level, the learner would stop responding, and the teacher would be told to continue if they didn't answer after a certain time.

The predictions of the number of teachers would would do the maximum voltage was a range of 0-3 out of 100, for an average of 1.2 out of 100. This poll was given to both colleagues and senior-year psychology students; both groups predicted roughly the same number. The actual results were astonishing with 65% of participants administering the maximum voltage despite the screams of the learner. Participants displayed signs of stress like sweating, trembling, nervous laughing, stuttering and even serizures. When replicated across the world, the results stayed nearly the same, and there was even an experiment done where the researchers were in an unmarked, poorly maintained office. These results were somewhat lowered, but still stayed close to the original results.

The Milgram Experiment has been called one of the most unethical Experiments of its time, due to the nature in which it was conducted.
